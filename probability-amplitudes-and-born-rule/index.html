Step 1: Complex Probability Amplitudes

In quantum mechanics, states are described by complex probability amplitudes. For a qubit: |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩, where α and β are complex numbers. The normalization condition requires |α|² + |β|² = 1. Unlike classical probabilities, these amplitudes can be negative or complex, enabling quantum interference.

|ψ⟩ = (αr + iαi)|0⟩ + (βr + iβi)|1⟩
Normalization: |α|² + |β|² = 1

Step 2: The Born Rule

The Born rule is the fundamental postulate that connects quantum amplitudes to classical probabilities. The probability of measuring outcome k is given by the squared magnitude of the amplitude: P(k) = |⟨k|ψ⟩|². This rule was proposed by Max Born in 1926 and is one of the core axioms of quantum mechanics.

P(k) = |ck|² = |⟨k|ψ⟩|²
where ck is the amplitude for state |k⟩

Step 3: From Amplitudes to Probabilities

For complex amplitudes α = αr + iαi, the probability is calculated as |α|² = αr² + αi². This converts complex amplitudes to real, non-negative probabilities that sum to 1. The complex phase of the amplitude affects interference but not individual measurement probabilities.

|α|² = (αr)² + (αi)²
P(0) = |α|², P(1) = |β|²
P(0) + P(1) = 1

Step 4: Quantum Interference

Probability amplitudes can interfere (constructively or destructively) due to their complex nature, leading to quantum interference effects. This is fundamentally different from classical probability: |α + β|² ≠ |α|² + |β|² in general. Only when we measure do we get classical probabilities via the Born rule.

Interference: |α + β|² ≠ |α|² + |β|²
Measurement: P = |amplitude|²
