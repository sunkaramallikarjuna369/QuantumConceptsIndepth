Step 1: Classical Impossibility vs Quantum Reality

Quantum tunneling is a purely quantum phenomenon with no classical analog. Classically, a particle with energy E cannot penetrate a potential barrier of height V₀ > E—it would be reflected 100%. However, in quantum mechanics, the wavefunction ψ(x) does not abruptly vanish at the barrier; instead, it decays exponentially inside the barrier as ψ(x) ∝ e^(-κx), where κ = √(2m(V₀-E)/ℏ²). If the barrier has finite width L, the wavefunction can "leak through" to the other side, giving a nonzero transmission probability T. This is tunneling—the particle has a probability T of appearing on the far side of the barrier, even though it lacks the classical energy to surmount it. Tunneling is not the particle "borrowing energy" or "going over the barrier"—it's a direct consequence of the wave nature of matter and the continuity of the wavefunction.

Classical: E < V₀ ⟹ T = 0 (total reflection)
Quantum: T ≈ e^(-2κL) where κ = √(2m(V₀-E)/ℏ²)
Wavefunction in barrier: ψ(x) ∝ e^(-κx) (exponential decay)

Step 2: Transmission Probability and WKB Approximation

For a rectangular barrier of height V₀ and width L, the exact transmission probability is T = [1 + (V₀²sinh²(κL))/(4E(V₀-E))]⁻¹ for E < V₀. For thick barriers (κL ≫ 1), this simplifies to T ≈ 16(E/V₀)(1 - E/V₀)e^(-2κL), showing exponential suppression with barrier width. The tunneling probability depends critically on three factors: (1) barrier height V₀ - E (higher barrier ⟹ lower T), (2) barrier width L (wider barrier ⟹ exponentially lower T), and (3) particle mass m (heavier particle ⟹ lower T). For arbitrary barrier shapes V(x), the WKB (Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin) approximation gives T ≈ exp(-2∫√(2m(V(x)-E)/ℏ²)dx), integrating over the classically forbidden region. This explains why electrons tunnel easily but macroscopic objects never do—the exponential suppression with mass is enormous.

Rectangular barrier: T ≈ 16(E/V₀)(1 - E/V₀)e^(-2κL)
WKB approximation: T ≈ exp(-2∫√(2m(V(x)-E)/ℏ²)dx)
Exponential dependence: T ∝ e^(-2√(2m(V₀-E))L/ℏ)

Step 3: Tunneling Time and Causality

How long does tunneling take? This is a subtle question. The Büttiker-Landauer traversal time τ = ℏ(dφ/dE), where φ is the phase shift of the transmitted wave, gives a characteristic timescale. For opaque barriers (T ≪ 1), τ saturates at τ ≈ L/v_barrier, where v_barrier = √(2(V₀-E)/m) is the "velocity" inside the barrier. Remarkably, τ can be shorter than L/c (speed of light), leading to apparent superluminal velocities. However, this does not violate causality—the peak of a wavepacket can travel faster than c, but no information or energy is transmitted superluminally. The "tunneling time paradox" arises from trying to assign a classical trajectory to a quantum process. Tunneling is fundamentally non-local—the particle does not have a well-defined position inside the barrier. Experiments with attosecond pulses have measured tunneling times, confirming quantum predictions.

Traversal time: τ = ℏ(dφ/dE) (phase-time relation)
Saturation: τ ≈ L/v_barrier for opaque barriers
No causality violation: Information speed ≤ c always

Step 4: Applications and Physical Phenomena

Tunneling is ubiquitous in nature and technology. Alpha decay: radioactive nuclei emit alpha particles via tunneling through the Coulomb barrier—Gamow's 1928 theory explained decay rates using tunneling. Scanning tunneling microscope (STM): electrons tunnel between a sharp tip and a surface, allowing atomic-resolution imaging (Nobel Prize 1986). Tunnel diodes: semiconductor devices exploit tunneling for ultra-fast switching. Nuclear fusion in stars: protons tunnel through the Coulomb repulsion barrier to fuse, powering the Sun—without tunneling, stars would not shine. Ammonia maser: the nitrogen atom tunnels through the plane of hydrogen atoms, creating a double-well potential used in the first maser (1954). Quantum computing: superconducting qubits use Josephson junctions, where Cooper pairs tunnel through an insulating barrier. Tunneling is also central to quantum field theory—vacuum decay, Hawking radiation, and instantons all involve tunneling processes.

Alpha decay: Decay rate Γ ∝ e^(-2G) (Gamow factor)
STM: Tunneling current I ∝ e^(-2κd) (exponential with distance)
Nuclear fusion: Rate ∝ e^(-E_G/kT) where E_G is Gamow energy
