Step 1: The Time-Dependent Schrödinger Equation

The Schrödinger equation is the fundamental equation of quantum mechanics, analogous to Newton's F=ma in classical mechanics. The time-dependent Schrödinger equation (TDSE) describes how quantum states evolve over time: iℏ∂|ψ⟩/∂t = Ĥ|ψ⟩, where Ĥ is the Hamiltonian operator (total energy operator), ℏ is the reduced Planck constant, and |ψ(t)⟩ is the state at time t. For a particle in a potential V(x), the Hamiltonian is Ĥ = -ℏ²/(2m)∇² + V(x), where the first term is kinetic energy and the second is potential energy. The TDSE is a linear partial differential equation—solutions can be superposed, which is the origin of quantum interference. This equation is deterministic: given |ψ(0)⟩, it uniquely determines |ψ(t)⟩ for all future times.

Time-dependent: iℏ∂ψ/∂t = Ĥψ = [-ℏ²/(2m)∇² + V(x)]ψ
Hamiltonian: Ĥ = T̂ + V̂ (kinetic + potential energy)
Solution: |ψ(t)⟩ = e^(-iĤt/ℏ)|ψ(0)⟩ (unitary time evolution)

Step 2: The Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation

For time-independent potentials V(x), we can separate variables: ψ(x,t) = φ(x)e^(-iEt/ℏ), where E is energy. Substituting into the TDSE gives the time-independent Schrödinger equation (TISE): Ĥφ(x) = Eφ(x). This is an eigenvalue problem: φ(x) are eigenstates (stationary states) with eigenvalues E (allowed energies). For bound states (particle confined to a region), E is quantized—only discrete energy levels are allowed. For example, in an infinite square well of width L, the allowed energies are Eₙ = n²π²ℏ²/(2mL²) for n=1,2,3,... The TISE is the foundation for understanding atomic spectra, molecular structure, and quantum chemistry. Solving it for different potentials V(x) reveals the quantum behavior of particles.

Time-independent: Ĥφ = Eφ (eigenvalue equation)
Stationary states: ψ(x,t) = φ(x)e^(-iEt/ℏ)
Energy quantization: E₁, E₂, E₃, ... (discrete for bound states)

Step 3: Physical Interpretation and Probability

The wavefunction ψ(x,t) itself is not directly observable—it's a complex-valued probability amplitude. The Born rule states that |ψ(x,t)|² gives the probability density of finding the particle at position x at time t. The total probability must be 1: ∫|ψ(x,t)|²dx = 1 (normalization condition). The Schrödinger equation preserves normalization: if ψ is normalized at t=0, it remains normalized for all t. This is because the equation is unitary (Ĥ is Hermitian). The wavefunction can spread, contract, or oscillate, but the total probability is conserved. Expectation values of observables are computed as ⟨Â⟩ = ⟨ψ|Â|ψ⟩. For example, the average position is ⟨x⟩ = ∫x|ψ(x)|²dx. The Schrödinger equation determines how these expectation values evolve in time.

Probability density: P(x,t) = |ψ(x,t)|²
Normalization: ∫|ψ(x,t)|²dx = 1 (conserved by TDSE)
Expectation value: ⟨Â⟩ = ∫ψ*Âψ dx

Step 4: Solutions for Common Potentials

Different potentials V(x) lead to different physical phenomena. Free particle (V=0): plane waves ψ(x) = e^(ikx) with continuous energy E = ℏ²k²/(2m). Step potential: partial reflection and transmission, even when E > V (quantum reflection). Barrier potential: quantum tunneling—particle can penetrate classically forbidden regions where E < V. Infinite square well: discrete energy levels Eₙ ∝ n², standing wave solutions. Harmonic oscillator V = ½mω²x²: equally spaced energy levels Eₙ = ℏω(n+½), Gaussian-like wavefunctions (Hermite polynomials). Hydrogen atom V = -e²/(4πε₀r): discrete bound states (n,l,m quantum numbers), explains atomic spectra. These solutions form the basis for understanding chemistry, solid-state physics, and quantum devices.

Free particle: E = ℏ²k²/(2m) (continuous spectrum)
Square well: Eₙ = n²π²ℏ²/(2mL²) (discrete, n=1,2,3,...)
Harmonic oscillator: Eₙ = ℏω(n+½) (equally spaced)
