Step 1: What is Hilbert Space?

A Hilbert space is a complete complex vector space equipped with an inner product. "Complete" means every Cauchy sequence converges to a point in the space (no "holes"). In quantum mechanics, the state of a quantum system is represented by a vector |ψ⟩ in a Hilbert space ℋ. For a single qubit, ℋ is 2-dimensional (ℂ²). For n qubits, ℋ is 2ⁿ-dimensional (ℂ^(2ⁿ)). The inner product ⟨φ|ψ⟩ measures the overlap between states. Hilbert space provides the mathematical framework for all of quantum mechanics—every quantum state, every observable, and every evolution lives in or acts on Hilbert space.

Hilbert space: ℋ (complete complex vector space with inner product)
Single qubit: ℋ = ℂ² (2D complex space = 4 real dimensions)
n qubits: ℋ = ℂ^(2ⁿ) (2ⁿ-dimensional complex space)

Step 2: Inner Product and Norm

The inner product ⟨φ|ψ⟩ on Hilbert space is a complex-valued function satisfying: (1) linearity in the second argument, (2) conjugate symmetry ⟨φ|ψ⟩* = ⟨ψ|φ⟩, (3) positive-definiteness ⟨ψ|ψ⟩ ≥ 0 with equality only for |ψ⟩ = 0. The norm ||ψ|| = √⟨ψ|ψ⟩ gives the "length" of a state vector. In quantum mechanics, states are normalized: ||ψ|| = 1, ensuring probabilities sum to 1. The inner product |⟨φ|ψ⟩|² gives the probability of measuring |φ⟩ when the system is in state |ψ⟩. Orthogonal states (⟨φ|ψ⟩ = 0) are perfectly distinguishable.

Inner product: ⟨φ|ψ⟩ = Σᵢ φᵢ*ψᵢ ∈ ℂ
Norm: ||ψ|| = √⟨ψ|ψ⟩ ∈ ℝ≥0
Normalization: ⟨ψ|ψ⟩ = 1 for physical states

Step 3: Basis and Dimensionality

A Hilbert space has a basis—a set of orthonormal vectors {|eᵢ⟩} such that ⟨eᵢ|eⱼ⟩ = δᵢⱼ (Kronecker delta) and any state can be written as |ψ⟩ = Σᵢ cᵢ|eᵢ⟩. For a single qubit, the computational basis is {|0⟩, |1⟩}. For two qubits, it's {|00⟩, |01⟩, |10⟩, |11⟩}. The dimension of the Hilbert space is the number of basis vectors. An n-qubit system has dimension 2ⁿ, which grows exponentially—this is why quantum computers can potentially solve certain problems exponentially faster than classical computers. Different bases (computational, Hadamard, Bell) provide different perspectives on the same quantum state.

Orthonormal basis: {|e₁⟩, |e₂⟩, ..., |eₙ⟩} with ⟨eᵢ|eⱼ⟩ = δᵢⱼ
Any state: |ψ⟩ = Σᵢ cᵢ|eᵢ⟩ where cᵢ = ⟨eᵢ|ψ⟩
Completeness: Σᵢ |eᵢ⟩⟨eᵢ| = I (identity operator)
