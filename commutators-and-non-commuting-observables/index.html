Step 2: Physical Meaning of Non-Commutation

When two observables don't commute, they represent incompatible measurements. You cannot prepare a quantum state that is simultaneously an eigenstate of both operators. The most famous example is position and momentum: [x̂,p̂] = iℏ. This non-zero commutator directly leads to the Heisenberg uncertainty principle ΔxΔp ≥ ℏ/2.

Step 3: Pauli Matrix Commutators

The Pauli matrices have well-defined commutation relations: [σ_x,σ_y] = 2iσ_z, [σ_y,σ_z] = 2iσ_x, and [σ_z,σ_x] = 2iσ_y. These relations form the Lie algebra of SU(2) and are fundamental to understanding spin and angular momentum in quantum mechanics. Each pair of Pauli operators is non-commuting.

[σ_x,σ_y] = 2iσ_z
[σ_y,σ_z] = 2iσ_x
[σ_z,σ_x] = 2iσ_y

Step 4: Simultaneous Eigenstates

Two operators can have simultaneous eigenstates if and only if they commute. This means you can find states that are definite (not superposed) with respect to both observables. For example, energy and angular momentum often commute in atomic systems, allowing states with definite values of both. Non-commuting operators cannot share a complete set of eigenstates.
