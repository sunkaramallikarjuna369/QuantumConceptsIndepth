Step 1: Quantum State Before Measurement

Before measurement, a quantum system exists in a superposition of basis states. For a qubit, this is represented as |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + βeiφ|1⟩, where α and β are complex probability amplitudes satisfying |α|² + |β|² = 1. The state exists in all possible configurations simultaneously until measured.

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + βeiφ|1⟩
where |α|² + |β|² = 1

Step 2: Born Rule and Measurement Probabilities

The Born rule states that the probability of measuring a particular eigenstate is the squared magnitude of its amplitude. For measuring in the computational basis {|0⟩, |1⟩}: P(0) = |α|² and P(1) = |β|². These probabilities are intrinsic to quantum mechanics and cannot be predicted with certainty for individual measurements.

P(0) = |α|² = |⟨0|ψ⟩|²
P(1) = |β|² = |⟨1|ψ⟩|²

Step 3: Wavefunction Collapse

Upon measurement, the quantum state instantaneously "collapses" to one of the measurement basis eigenstates. This is a non-unitary, irreversible process that fundamentally distinguishes quantum measurement from unitary evolution. The superposition is destroyed, and the system is left in a definite state corresponding to the measurement outcome.

Before: |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ (superposition)
After: |0⟩ or |1⟩ (definite state)

Step 4: Measurement in Different Bases

The measurement outcome depends on the choice of measurement basis. Measuring in the X-basis {|+⟩, |−⟩} or Y-basis {|↻⟩, |↺⟩} gives different probabilities than the Z-basis. This demonstrates that measurement is not simply revealing pre-existing properties, but actively affecting the quantum state.

X-basis: |+⟩ = (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2, |−⟩ = (|0⟩ − |1⟩)/√2
Y-basis: |↻⟩ = (|0⟩ + i|1⟩)/√2, |↺⟩ = (|0⟩ − i|1⟩)/√2
