Step 1: Complex Numbers Basics

A complex number z = a + bi has a real part a and an imaginary part b, where i = √(-1) is the imaginary unit satisfying i² = -1. Complex numbers extend the real number line into a two-dimensional complex plane, where the horizontal axis represents real parts and the vertical axis represents imaginary parts. Every complex number can be represented as a point or vector in this plane. Complex numbers are essential in quantum mechanics because probability amplitudes are complex-valued, allowing for interference effects that have no classical analog.

z = a + bi where a, b ∈ ℝ and i² = -1
Re(z) = a (real part)
Im(z) = b (imaginary part)

Step 2: Polar Form and Euler's Formula

Any complex number can be written in polar form: z = r·e^(iθ) = r(cos θ + i sin θ), where r = |z| = √(a² + b²) is the magnitude (modulus) and θ = arg(z) = arctan(b/a) is the phase (argument). Euler's formula e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ is one of the most beautiful equations in mathematics and is fundamental to quantum mechanics. The polar form makes multiplication and division of complex numbers geometrically intuitive: multiplying by e^(iθ) rotates by angle θ in the complex plane.

z = r·e^(iθ) = r(cos θ + i sin θ) (polar form)
|z| = √(a² + b²) (magnitude)
arg(z) = arctan(b/a) (phase)

Step 3: Complex Conjugate and Operations

The complex conjugate of z = a + bi is z* = a - bi (flip the sign of the imaginary part). The conjugate is crucial in quantum mechanics: the probability of measuring a state |ψ⟩ is |⟨φ|ψ⟩|² = ⟨φ|ψ⟩*⟨φ|ψ⟩. Key properties: |z|² = z·z*, (z*)* = z, and (z₁ + z₂)* = z₁* + z₂*. Operations on complex numbers follow algebraic rules: addition is component-wise, multiplication uses i² = -1, and division requires multiplying by the conjugate.

z* = a - bi (complex conjugate)
|z|² = z·z* = a² + b²
z₁·z₂ = (a₁ + ib₁)(a₂ + ib₂) = (a₁a₂ - b₁b₂) + i(a₁b₂ + a₂b₁)

Step 4: Vector Spaces in Quantum Mechanics

Quantum states live in a complex vector space called Hilbert space. A vector space over complex numbers is a set where you can add vectors and multiply them by complex scalars, satisfying axioms like associativity and distributivity. The quantum state |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ is a vector in a 2D complex vector space (4 real dimensions!). Inner products ⟨φ|ψ⟩ are complex-valued and satisfy ⟨φ|ψ⟩* = ⟨ψ|φ⟩. The complex structure enables quantum interference: amplitudes can add constructively or destructively, unlike classical probabilities.

Vector space: V over ℂ with addition and scalar multiplication
Inner product: ⟨φ|ψ⟩ ∈ ℂ with ⟨φ|ψ⟩* = ⟨ψ|φ⟩
Quantum state: |ψ⟩ = Σ c_i|i⟩ where c_i ∈ ℂ
