Step 1: Bell States

Bell states are maximally entangled two-qubit states that form an orthonormal basis:

|Φ⁺⟩ = (|00⟩ + |11⟩)/√2
|Φ⁻⟩ = (|00⟩ − |11⟩)/√2
|Ψ⁺⟩ = (|01⟩ + |10⟩)/√2
|Ψ⁻⟩ = (|01⟩ − |10⟩)/√2

Step 2: Entanglement Properties

Entangled states cannot be written as a product of individual qubit states:

|Φ⁺⟩ ≠ |ψ_A⟩ ⊗ |ψ_B⟩

This means the qubits are fundamentally correlated and cannot be described independently.

Step 3: Measurement Correlations

When measuring entangled qubits, the outcomes are correlated. For |Φ⁺⟩ in the Z-basis:

P(00) = 1/2, P(11) = 1/2
P(01) = 0, P(10) = 0

The qubits always give the same result (both 0 or both 1).

Step 4: Bell's Theorem

Bell's theorem shows that quantum entanglement produces correlations that cannot be explained by local hidden variables. The CHSH inequality:

|⟨AB⟩ + ⟨AB'⟩ + ⟨A'B⟩ − ⟨A'B'⟩| ≤ 2 (classical)
Can reach 2√2 ≈ 2.828 (quantum)
