Step 1: What is a Tensor Product?

The tensor product (⊗) is a mathematical operation that combines two vector spaces into a larger vector space. For quantum systems, if qubit 1 is in state |ψ₁⟩ in space ℋ₁ and qubit 2 is in state |ψ₂⟩ in space ℋ₂, the combined two-qubit system lives in the tensor product space ℋ₁ ⊗ ℋ₂. If |ψ₁⟩ = α₁|0⟩ + β₁|1⟩ and |ψ₂⟩ = α₂|0⟩ + β₂|1⟩, then |ψ₁⟩ ⊗ |ψ₂⟩ = α₁α₂|00⟩ + α₁β₂|01⟩ + β₁α₂|10⟩ + β₁β₂|11⟩. The tensor product is how we mathematically describe composite quantum systems—it's the foundation for multi-qubit quantum computing.

Tensor product: |ψ₁⟩ ⊗ |ψ₂⟩ (combines two systems)
Dimension: dim(ℋ₁ ⊗ ℋ₂) = dim(ℋ₁) × dim(ℋ₂)
Two qubits: ℂ² ⊗ ℂ² = ℂ⁴ (4-dimensional space)

Step 2: Separable vs Entangled States

A two-qubit state is separable (or product state) if it can be written as |ψ⟩ = |ψ₁⟩ ⊗ |ψ₂⟩ for some single-qubit states |ψ₁⟩ and |ψ₂⟩. For example, |+⟩ ⊗ |0⟩ = (1/√2)(|0⟩ + |1⟩) ⊗ |0⟩ = (1/√2)(|00⟩ + |10⟩) is separable. However, the Bell state |Φ⁺⟩ = (1/√2)(|00⟩ + |11⟩) cannot be written as a tensor product of two single-qubit states—it's entangled. Entangled states exhibit quantum correlations that have no classical analog. Most states in ℋ₁ ⊗ ℋ₂ are entangled; separable states are a tiny subset.

Separable: |ψ⟩ = |ψ₁⟩ ⊗ |ψ₂⟩ (can factor into single-qubit states)
Entangled: |ψ⟩ ≠ |ψ₁⟩ ⊗ |ψ₂⟩ for any |ψ₁⟩, |ψ₂⟩
Bell state: |Φ⁺⟩ = (|00⟩ + |11⟩)/√2 (maximally entangled)

Step 3: Computing Tensor Products

To compute |ψ₁⟩ ⊗ |ψ₂⟩, multiply each component of |ψ₁⟩ by each component of |ψ₂⟩. If |ψ₁⟩ = (a, b)ᵀ and |ψ₂⟩ = (c, d)ᵀ, then |ψ₁⟩ ⊗ |ψ₂⟩ = (ac, ad, bc, bd)ᵀ. For basis states: |0⟩ ⊗ |0⟩ = |00⟩, |0⟩ ⊗ |1⟩ = |01⟩, |1⟩ ⊗ |0⟩ = |10⟩, |1⟩ ⊗ |1⟩ = |11⟩. The tensor product is bilinear: (c|ψ₁⟩) ⊗ |ψ₂⟩ = c(|ψ₁⟩ ⊗ |ψ₂⟩) and (|ψ₁⟩ + |φ₁⟩) ⊗ |ψ₂⟩ = |ψ₁⟩ ⊗ |ψ₂⟩ + |φ₁⟩ ⊗ |ψ₂⟩. This linearity is crucial for quantum computation.

Component-wise: (a|0⟩ + b|1⟩) ⊗ (c|0⟩ + d|1⟩) = ac|00⟩ + ad|01⟩ + bc|10⟩ + bd|11⟩
Bilinearity: (c|ψ⟩) ⊗ |φ⟩ = c(|ψ⟩ ⊗ |φ⟩)
Distributivity: (|ψ⟩ + |φ⟩) ⊗ |χ⟩ = |ψ⟩⊗|χ⟩ + |φ⟩⊗|χ⟩

Step 4: Operators on Tensor Product Spaces

Operators on composite systems are also tensor products. A single-qubit gate Û acting on qubit 1 only is represented as Û ⊗ I (identity on qubit 2). For example, the Hadamard on qubit 1 is H ⊗ I. Two-qubit gates like CNOT cannot be written as tensor products—they create entanglement. The action of Â ⊗ B̂ on |ψ⟩ ⊗ |φ⟩ is (Â ⊗ B̂)(|ψ⟩ ⊗ |φ⟩) = (Â|ψ⟩) ⊗ (B̂|φ⟩). This structure allows us to build complex quantum circuits from simple gates acting on individual qubits or pairs of qubits.
